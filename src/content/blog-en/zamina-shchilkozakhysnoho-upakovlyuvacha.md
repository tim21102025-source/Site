The seal is one of the most important but most vulnerable parts of a vinyl window. Its condition directly affects tightness, insulation, and soundproofing. A worn seal leads to leaks, drafts, increased heating costs, and mold growth. Timely seal replacement is a simple procedure that prevents more serious problems. In Kyiv, where winter temperatures can drop to -15°C, window tightness is critically important.

## Signs of a worn seal

You can identify that a seal needs replacement by several signs:

- **Cracks and tears** — the most obvious sign. The rubber becomes brittle and cracks, especially at bend points.
- **Shape distortion** — the seal becomes flat, loses its round cross-section, and no longer fully fills the groove.
- **Darkening or yellowing** — color change indicates material aging.
- **Sticking to the frame** — when opening the sash, the seal sticks and stretches instead of detaching freely.
- **Winter drafts** — even with normal window adjustment, you feel cold air.
- **Condensation on the glass edge** — may indicate a worn glass unit edge seal.

> **Tip:** Do a simple test — press a sheet of paper between the sash and frame, close the window. If the paper pulls out too easily or tears — the clamping is insufficient, and the cause may be in the seal.

## Types of seals

Several types of seals are available on the market, differing in material, shape, and properties:

### By material:

**EPDM (ethylene propylene diene monomer)**
- The most common material for modern windows.
- Temperature resistance from -40°C to +120°C.
- Resistant to UV radiation and atmospheric effects.
- Lifespan: 10-15 years.

**Silicone**
- The most elastic and longest-lasting.
- Doesn't lose elasticity at extreme temperatures.
- Higher price compared to EPDM.
- Lifespan: 15-20 years.

**TPE (thermoplastic elastomer)**
- A compromise between price and quality.
- Less elastic than silicone, but better than regular rubber.
- Lifespan: 8-12 years.

### By cross-section shape:

- **D-shaped** — the most common for sash seals.
- **E-shaped** — for increased clamping.
- **P-shaped** (cap) — for mounting on the sash or frame edge.
- **Ring-shaped** — for certain glass unit types.

> **Important:** When replacing seals, always use the same type and cross-section as the original installed by the manufacturer. The wrong choice can worsen tightness or make the window difficult to open.

## Step-by-step replacement guide

Seal replacement is a process you can do yourself with a minimal set of tools.

### Tools and materials:

- New seal (appropriate type and size).
- Flathead screwdriver or wooden spatula.
- Knife or scissors.
- Silicone seal lubricant.
- Soapy solution for cleaning the groove.

### Replacing the sash seal:

1. **Open the sash** and find the seal end — it's usually in the upper corner.
2. **Pull out the old seal** — simply pull it toward you, it comes out of the groove easily. If the seal is glued, gently pry it with a flathead screwdriver.
3. **Clean the groove** — wipe the groove with a damp cloth and soapy solution. Make sure no old adhesive residue or dirt remains in the groove.
4. **Install the new seal** — start from the upper corner, inserting the seal into the groove gradually. Don't stretch it too tight — the seal should lie loosely, without folds.
5. **Join the ends** — cut the seal so the ends meet without gaps and without overlap. Some manufacturers recommend cutting the ends at a 45° angle for a better joint.
6. **Treat with lubricant** — apply a thin layer of silicone lubricant to the new seal. This eases sliding and extends its lifespan.

### Replacing the frame seal:

The process is similar, but the seal is on the frame, not the sash. When replacing, pay attention to areas where the seal passes through the hinges — there may be mounting elements that need to be carefully worked around.

> **Tip:** Don't cut the seal too short. It's better to leave a small margin (2-3mm) that can be trimmed after checking.

## Seal maintenance

Proper care significantly extends the seal's life and delays the need for replacement:

### What to do:

- **Wash with soapy solution** every 4-6 months. Use a soft cloth and neutral soap.
- **Treat with silicone lubricant** after each wash. This maintains elasticity and prevents sticking.
- **Clean off dirt** — dust, sand, and small particles act as abrasives and accelerate wear.
- **Check condition** during seasonal window maintenance.

### What to avoid:

- **Solvents and harsh chemicals** — gasoline, acetone, WD-40 destroy the rubber structure.
- **Paints and varnishes** — completely destroy the seal's elasticity.
- **Mechanical friction** — don't wipe the seal with rough materials.
- **Adding extra seals** — "doubling" the seal doesn't improve tightness, but rather worsens clamping and causes deformation.

> **Important:** If the seal sticks to the frame and tears when opening — this is a sign that it needs replacement, not lubrication.

## When to call a professional

Although seal replacement is a fairly simple procedure, there are situations where it's better to trust a professional:

- **Glass unit seal replacement** — this requires removing the glass unit, which risks damage.
- **Complex window geometry** — non-standard shapes, arches, round windows.
- **Simultaneous seal and hardware replacement** — complex repairs are best left to a professional.
- **Window at great height** — safety comes first.
- **Mismatched seal type** — if you're unsure which seal you need.

> **Professional tip:** Always buy seals with a margin — at least 1 extra meter. If you make a cutting error or find a material defect, you won't need to go back to the store.
